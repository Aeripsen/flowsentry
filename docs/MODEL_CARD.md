# Model card: FlowSentry TwoStageRejectClassifier

Version 0.1.0 (Week 1 milestone: NSL-KDD benchmark reproduction). All numbers on this page are
measured, come from `artifacts/metrics.json`, and are regenerated by every run of
`python -m flowsentry.train`.

## Model details

- **Architecture:** two-stage hierarchical classifier with a tunable reject (abstain) option,
  from my SECRYPT 2026 paper on hierarchical UDP/QUIC intrusion detection.
  - **Stage 1:** RandomForest (60 trees, `class_weight="balanced_subsample"`) on a cheap feature
    subset. In this benchmark reproduction the subset is the first half of the NSL-KDD vector; in
    the headline system (Week 2) the stages run genuinely different feature sets produced by my
    UDPFlowLyzer / QUICFlowLyzer extractors.
  - **Escalation:** flows where stage 1 confidence is below 0.90 escalate to **stage 2**, a
    RandomForest (200 trees) on the full feature vector. Measured escalation rate on KDDTest+:
    47.5%.
  - **Reject:** after the final stage, flows with confidence below the reject threshold return
    `unknown` instead of a label. The threshold is a request-time parameter, not baked in.
- **Confidence:** RandomForest vote fractions. These are not calibrated probabilities; the
  coverage-reliability curve is measured directly, but a given threshold value does not transfer
  to other datasets.
- **Implementation:** `src/flowsentry/model.py`, scikit-learn, seeded (`random_state=42`).

## Intended use

- Classify network flows into 5 categories (dos, normal, probe, r2l, u2r) with an explicit
  abstain path, so uncertain flows can be routed to a human or a deeper detector instead of
  receiving a low-confidence guess.
- Demonstrate and study the coverage-vs-reliability trade-off of a reject option in flow-based
  intrusion detection.

**Out of scope:** this Week 1 artifact is a benchmark reproduction. It is not trained on modern
traffic and must not be pointed at a production network as-is. NSL-KDD features are derived from
1998-era DARPA traffic; their semantics do not transfer to modern UDP/QUIC traffic.

## Data

- **Dataset:** NSL-KDD public benchmark (Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity), fetched by
  `scripts/get_data.py`.
- **Split:** the official KDDTrain+ (125,973 rows) / KDDTest+ (22,544 rows) files. KDDTest+
  contains attack subtypes absent from training, so the split is leakage-safe and novelty-aware
  by construction. The one-hot preprocessor is fit on train only
  (`OneHotEncoder(handle_unknown="ignore")` absorbs unseen categories at test time).
- **Features:** 122 after one-hot encoding of `protocol_type`, `service`, `flag`; the `label` and
  `difficulty` columns are dropped from the feature set.
- **Labels:** the standard NSL-KDD attack-name to 5-class mapping (`src/flowsentry/data.py`).

## Evaluation results (measured)

**Full coverage (reject threshold 0, no abstain): accuracy 71.5% (0.7148), macro-F1 0.457.**

| Class | F1 |
|---|---|
| dos | 0.8091 |
| normal | 0.7558 |
| probe | 0.6764 |
| r2l | 0.0151 |
| u2r | 0.0286 |

**Coverage vs reliability** (reliability = accuracy on the answered subset; escalation rate
0.4746 at every threshold since escalation happens before rejection):

| Reject threshold | Coverage | Reliability | n answered |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.00 | 1.000 | 0.7148 | 22,544 |
| 0.50 | 0.9901 | 0.7181 | 22,320 |
| 0.60 | 0.9665 | 0.7307 | 21,788 |
| 0.70 | 0.9430 | 0.7446 | 21,260 |
| 0.80 | 0.8974 | 0.7555 | 20,230 |
| 0.90 | 0.8203 | 0.8103 | 18,493 |
| 0.95 | 0.3164 | 0.8311 | 7,133 |
| 0.99 | 0.2473 | 0.9464 | 5,575 |

Reading it: answering everything gives 71.5% reliability. Keeping 82% of flows lifts the answered
subset to 81%. At the strictest setting the model answers only the 24.7% of flows it is most sure
about and is right 94.6% of the time; the rest are handed off as `unknown` rather than guessed.

## Why the full-coverage numbers look low (and why they are reported anyway)

NSL-KDD's r2l and u2r classes are famously hard: they are a tiny fraction of training data and the
test set introduces attack subtypes never seen in training. Near-zero F1 on them at full coverage
is the known, honest result on the official split. Much of the 99%+ literature and notebook work
on this dataset evaluates on a random split of the training file, which leaks and says nothing
about novel attacks. This project reports the official-split numbers and treats the reject option
as the principled response: abstain on the uncertain rare-class flows instead of emitting a
confident wrong label.

## Limitations (honest)

1. **Rare classes are effectively not detected at full coverage.** r2l F1 0.0151, u2r F1 0.0286.
   The reject option mitigates the damage (uncertain flows abstain instead of mislabeling) but
   does not fix detection of these classes.
2. **Benchmark, not the headline data.** Nothing here says anything about performance on modern
   UDP/QUIC or cloud-DDoS traffic. The headline evaluation (my own extractors +
   BCCC-UDP-Cloud-DDoS-2024) is the Week 2 roadmap and does not exist yet.
3. **Single-dataset evaluation.** No cross-dataset generalization test has been run.
4. **No adversarial evaluation yet.** Whether the reject option catches deliberately perturbed
   flows is a hypothesis until the Week 3 adversarial probe measures it. See
   `docs/THREAT_MODEL.md`.
5. **Stages share one feature vector.** In this reproduction the two stages are subsets of the
   same NSL-KDD vector; the genuinely-different-extractors design (UDP-only vs QUIC-augmented) is
   Week 2.
6. **Uncalibrated confidence.** Thresholds are meaningful only against the measured curve for
   this dataset.
7. **Latency and throughput are unmeasured.** To be measured under load (Week 3).
